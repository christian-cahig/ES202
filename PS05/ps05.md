## Problem I

Find the power series solution (in $x$) of the following ODE:

$$
y^{\prime \prime} + 2 x y^{\prime} + y = 0
$$ (1.0)

We assume that the solution $y$ is a power series solution:

$$
y = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} a_{m} x^{m}
$$ (1.1)
where the $a_{n}$ terms are constant coefficients.
Differentiating $y$ with respect to $x$,

$$
y^{\prime} = \sum_{m=1}^{\infty} m a_{m} x^{m-1}
\\
y^{\prime \prime} = \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} m \left(m - 1\right) a_{m} x^{m-2}
$$ (1.2)
Substituting (1.2) onto (1.0),

$$
\sum_{m=2}^{\infty} m \left(m - 1\right) a_{m} x^{m-2}
+
2 \sum_{m=1}^{\infty} m a_{m} x^{m}
+
\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} a_{m} x^{m}
= 0
$$
The first series can be re-written
in terms of $x^{m}$, *i.e.*,

$$
\sum_{m=2}^{\infty} m \left(m - 1\right) a_{m} x^{m-2}
=
\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \left(m + 2\right) \left(m + 1\right) a_{m+2} x^{m}
$$
Moreover, notice that the index of second series in (1.3) can start at $m = 0$:

$$
\sum_{m=1}^{\infty} m a_{m} x^{m}
=
0 \cdot a_{0} x^{0} + 1 \cdot a_{1} x^{1} + 2 \cdot a_{2} x^{2} + \cdots
=
\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} m a_{m} x^{m}
$$
The series equivalent of the given ODE can now be expressed
in terms of $x^{m}$ and with indices starting at 0:

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}
\left[\left(m + 2\right) \left(m + 1\right) a_{m+2} + \left(2m + 1\right) a_{m}\right]
x^{m} =0
$$ (1.3)
The homogeneity of the given ODE means that the corresponding coefficient
of $x^{m}$ in (1.3) is zero, that is,

$$
a_{m+2} = -
\frac{2m + 1}{\left(m + 1\right) \left(m + 2\right)}
a_{m},
\quad m = 0,1,2,\ldots
$$ (1.4a)
Note that $a_{0}$ and $a_{1}$ cannot be determined from the recurrence relation alone,
and hence in the present treatment will be as arbitrary constants
derived from additional information (*e.g.*, initial conditions).
Moreover, the recurrence relation can be "split" into odd and even terms:

$$
a_{2k} = \left(-1\right)^{k} a_{0}
\prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \frac{4j + 1}{\left(2j + 1\right) \left(2j + 2\right)},
\quad k = 1,2,3,\ldots
$$ (1.4b)
$$
a_{2k+1} = \left(-1\right)^{k} a_{1}
\prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \frac{4j + 3}{\left(2j + 2\right) \left(2j + 3\right)},
\quad k = 1,2,3,\ldots
$$ (1.4c)
Therefore, we can write the series solution to (1.0) as

$$
y =
a_{0} \left[
    1 + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \left(-1\right)^{k}
    \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \frac{4j + 1}{\left(2j + 1\right) \left(2j + 2\right)} x^{2k}
\right]
+
a_{1} \left[
    x + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \left(-1\right)^{k}
    \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \frac{4j + 3}{\left(2j + 2\right) \left(2j + 3\right)} x^{2k+1}
\right]
$$ (1.5)
where $a_{0}$ and $a_{1}$ are constants.

## Problem II

Find the power series solution (in $x$) of the following ODE:

$$
y^{\prime \prime} - 4 x y^{\prime} + \left(4 x^{2} - 2\right) y = 0
$$ (2.0)

We assume that the solution $y$ is a power series solution:

$$
y = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} a_{m} x^{m}
$$ (2.1)
where the $a_{n}$ terms are constant coefficients.
Differentiating $y$ with respect to $x$,

$$
y^{\prime} = \sum_{m=1}^{\infty} m a_{m} x^{m-1}
\\
y^{\prime \prime} = \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} m \left(m - 1\right) a_{m} x^{m-2}
$$ (2.2)
Substituting (2.2) onto (2.0),

$$
\sum_{m=2}^{\infty} m \left(m - 1\right) a_{m} x^{m-2}
- 4 \sum_{m=1}^{\infty} m a_{m} x^{m}
+ 4 \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} m a_{m} x^{m+2}
- 2 \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} m a_{m} x^{m}
= 0
$$ (2.1a)

Expressing all series terms (2.1a) in $x^{m}$,

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \left(m + 2\right) \left(m + 1\right) a_{m+2} x^{m}
- 4 \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} m a_{m} x^{m}
+ 4 \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} a_{m-2} x^{m}
- 2 \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} a_{m} x^{m}
= 0
$$ (2.1b)
Note that

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \left(m + 2\right) \left(m + 1\right) a_{m+2} x^{m}
= 2 a_{2} + 6 a_{3} x
+ \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} \left(m + 2\right) \left(m + 1\right) a_{m+2} x^{m}
$$

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} m a_{m} x^{m}
= a_{1} x + \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} m a_{m} x^{m}
$$

$$
\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} a_{m} x^{m}
= a_{0} + a_{1} x + \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} a_{m} x^{m}
$$
Hence, we can rewrite the series terms in (2.1b) to have indices starting at $m=2$:

$$
2a_{2} + 6a_{3}x - 6a_{1}x - 2a_{0}
+ \sum_{m=2}^{\infty}
\left[ \left(m+2\right)\left(m+1\right) a_{m+2} - \left(4m+2\right) a_{m} + 4 a_{m-2} \right]
x^{m} = 0
$$ (2.1c)

Since (2.0) is homogenous, it follows from (2.1c) that

$$
2a_{2} - 2a_{0} = 0 \Longrightarrow a_{2} = a_{0}
$$ (2.2a)
$$
6a_{3} - 6a_{1} = 0 \Longrightarrow a_{3} = a_{1}
$$ (2.2b)
$$
\left(m+2\right)\left(m+1\right) a_{m+2} - \left(4m+2\right) a_{m} + 4 a_{m-2} = 0
\Longrightarrow
a_{m+2} = \frac{\left(4m+2\right) a_{m} - 4 a_{m-2}}{\left(m+1\right)\left(m+2\right)}
$$ (2.2c)
Now, with $m=2,3,4,5,6,7,9$,

$$
a_{4} = \frac{a_{0}}{2},\ 
a_{6} = \frac{a_{0}}{6},\ 
a_{8} = \frac{a_{0}}{24};
\quad
a_{5} = \frac{a_{1}}{2},\ 
a_{7} = \frac{a_{1}}{6},\ 
a_{9} = \frac{a_{1}}{24}
$$
This suggests two patterns for when even and odd $m$'s:

$$
a_{0},\ a_{2} = a_{0},\ a_{4} = \frac{a_{0}}{2},\ a_{6} = \frac{a_{0}}{6},\ 
a_{8} = \frac{a_{0}}{24},\ 
\cdots\ \Longrightarrow\ 
a_{k} = \frac{a_{0}}{\left(2k\right)!},
\quad k=0,1,2,\ldots
\ \ \ \ \ \ \ 
$$

$$
a_{1},\ a_{3} = a_{1},\  a_{5} = \frac{a_{1}}{2},\  a_{7} = \frac{a_{1}}{6},\ 
a_{9} = \frac{a_{1}}{24},\ 
\cdots\ \Longrightarrow\ 
a_{k} = \frac{a_{1}}{ \left(2k + 1\right) !},
\quad k=0,1,2,\ldots
$$
Notice that any $a_{m}$ is dependent on either $a_{0}$ or $a_{1}$,
but $a_{0}$ and $a_{1}$ cannot be determined solely from the recurrence relations.
We can treat $a_{0}$ and $a_{1}$ as yet unknown constants
whereupon the coeffiecents of two different power series are based.
Therefore, the power series solution to (2.0) is

$$
y =
\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{a_{0}}{\left(2m\right)!} x^{2m}
+
\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{a_{1}}{\left(2m+1\right)!} x^{2m+1}
=
a_{0} \cosh x + a_{1} \sinh x
$$ (2.3)
where $a_{0}$ and $a_{1}$ are constants.

## Problem IV

Find a basis of solutions for the following ODE.
Try to identify the series as expansions of known functions.

$$
x^{2} y^{\prime \prime} + 2 x^{3} y^{\prime} + \left(x^{2} - 2\right) y = 0
$$ (4.0)

Note that (4.0) can be rewritten as

$$
\left(x - \alpha\right)^{2} \tilde{a} \left(x\right) y^{\prime \prime}
+
\left(x - \alpha\right) \tilde{b} \left(x\right) y^{\prime}
+
\tilde{c} \left(x\right) y
= 0
$$ (4.1)
where $\alpha = 0$, $\tilde{a} \left(x\right) = 1$,
$\tilde{b} \left(x\right) = 2x^{2}$, and
$\tilde{c} \left(x\right) = x^{2} - 2$.
With $\tilde{a} \left(\alpha\right) \neq 0$,
$x = \alpha = 0$ is a regular singular point of (4.0).
The indicial equation would then be

$$
r^{2} +
\left(\frac{\tilde{b} \left(\alpha\right)}{\tilde{a} \left(\alpha\right)} - 1\right) r
+
\frac{\tilde{c} \left(\alpha\right)}{\tilde{a} \left(\alpha\right)}
= 0
\Longrightarrow
r^{2} - r -2 = 0
$$ (4.2)
whose roots are $r_{1} = -1$ and $r_{2} = 2$.

We look for a Frobenius series solution to (4.0) of the form

$$
y = \left(x - \alpha\right)^{r}
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_{k} \left(x - \alpha\right)^{k}
=
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_{k} x^{k+r}
$$ (4.3)
where $r$ is either of the roots of the indicial equation.
Differentiating with respect to $x$,

$$
y^{\prime} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}
\left(k+r\right) a_{k} x^{k+r-1}
$$ (4.4a)
$$
y^{\prime \prime} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}
\left(k+r\right) \left(k+r-1\right) a_{k} x^{k+r-2}
$$ (4.4b)
Substituting (4.3) and (4.4) to (4.0),

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(k+r\right) \left(k+r-1\right) a_{k} x^{k+r}
+
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} 2 \left(k+r\right) a_{k} x^{k+r+2}
+
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_{k} x^{k+r+2}
-
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} 2 a_{k} x^{k+r}
= 0
$$
which, upon rearranging, becomes

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left[\left(k+r\right) \left(k+r-1\right) - 2\right] a_{k} x^{k+r}
+
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left[2 \left(k+r\right) + 1\right] a_{k} x^{k+r+2}
= 0
$$ (4.5)
The homogeneity of the given ODE also means that
the coefficient of each power of $x$ in the left hand side of (4.5) equals zero.
The first two terms of the first sum involve $x^{r}$ and $x^{r+1}$,
while the second sum starts with $x^{r+2}$.
The respective coefficients of $x^{r}$ and $x^{r+1}$ are

$$
\left[r \left(r-1\right) - 2\right] a_{0}
$$ (4.6a)
$$
\left[r \left(r+1\right) - 2\right] a_{1}
$$ (4.6b)
Since $r$ must satisfy the indicial equation (4.2),
then $r \left(r-1\right) - 2 = 0$, *i.e.*,
the entire expression (4.6a) amounts to zero.
This also means that the value of $a_{0}$
could be arbitrary, based on the conditions
on the coefficients of the other powers of $x$.
Moreover, equating (4.6b) to zero implies that

$$
a_{1} = 0
$$ (4.7)

We now need to derive conditions on the coefficients
of the other powers of $x$,
First, by introducing a new index variable $n = k+2$,
the second sum in (4.5) can be written as

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left[2 \left(k+r\right) + 1\right] a_{k} x^{k+r+2}
=
\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \left[2\left(n+r\right) - 3\right] a_{n-2} x^{n+r}
$$ (4.8a)
Owing to the indicial equation and to (4.7),
we can set the starting index of the first sum in (4.5) to $2$;
then without losing meaning,
we can rename the summation index to $n$,

$$
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left[\left(k+r\right) \left(k+r-1\right) - 2\right] a_{k} x^{k+r}
=
\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \left[\left(n+r\right)^{2} -n-r- 2\right] a_{n} x^{n+r}
$$ (4.8b)
Substituting (4.8a)-(4.8b) onto (4.5) and simplifying,

$$
\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \left\{
    \left[\left(n+r\right)^{2}-n-r-2\right] a_{n}
    +
    \left[2\left(n+r\right) - 3\right] a_{n-2}
\right\} x^{n+r}
= 0
$$ (4.9)
By homogeneity, the coefficient of each $x^{n+r}$ term must vanish, *i.e.*,

$$
\left[\left(n+r\right)^{2}-n-r-2\right] a_{n}
+
\left[2\left(n+r\right) - 3\right] a_{n-2}
= 0,
\quad n = 2,3,4,\ldots
$$
or, more explicitly as a recurrence relation,

$$
a_{n} = - \frac{2\left(n+r\right)-3}{\left(n+r\right)^{2}-n-r-2} a_{n-2},
\quad n = 2,3,4,\ldots
$$
As earlier noted, $a_{0}$ is arbitray and $a_{1} = 0$ (4.7).
This means that for even values of $n$, $a_{n}$ is proportional to $a_{0}$,
and that for odd values of $n$, $a_{n} = 0$.
We can thus rewrite the recurrence relation to explicitly drop odd $n$'s:

$$
a_{2m} = \left(-1\right)^{m} a_{0}
\prod_{j=1}^{m}
\frac{2\left(2j+r\right)-3}{\left(2j+r\right)^{2}-2j-r-2},
\quad m = 1,2,3,\ldots
$$ (4.10)
where $a_{0}$ is a constant.

By using the roots of the indicial equation (4.2),
we can identify two solutions to (4.0).
For $r = r_{1} = -1$,

$$
y_{1} = a_{0} x^{-1} + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} a_{2k} x^{2k-1}
\\
a_{2k} = \left(-1\right)^{k} a_{0}
\prod_{j=1}^{m}
\frac{4j-5}{\left(2j-1\right)^{2}-\left(2j+1\right)}
$$ (4.11)
For $r = r_{2} = 2$,

$$
y_{2} = a_{0} x^{2} + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} a_{2k} x^{2k+2}
\\
a_{2k} = \left(-1\right)^{k} a_{0}
\prod_{j=1}^{m}
\frac{4j+1}{4\left(j+1\right)^{2}-2\left(j+2\right)}
$$ (4.12)
Observe that $y_{1}$ and $y_{2}$ are linearly independent.
Hence, $y_{1}$ and $y_{2}$ constitute a basis of solutions to (4.0).
